The thesis

The dating market is one of the last major consumer categories where product innovation has stagnated for 15 years, where regulatory and cultural pressure is rising, and where a new generation is ready to pay for something better.

Three forces converge right now to make an actor like ALMA possible:

First, a saturation of existing models. Unsubscribe rates on the major apps are exploding. Younger generations openly express their exhaustion. People aged 18-30 massively declare wanting a long-term relationship, while apps continue to optimize for infinite scroll.

Second, structural regulatory pressure. The DSA, the AI Act, and the rise of litigation linked to behaviors on dating apps create a barrier to entry that favors compliant-by-design European actors.

Finally, a visible cultural shift. Slow dating, the return to real-world meetings, the mainstream critique of the attention economy are no longer niche trends. They have become living-room conversations.

Match Group cannot pivot without sacrificing several billion dollars of annual revenue. The space is open for an actor who takes the opposite angle.

Why us, why now

01

A structural competitive moat

Match Group generates revenue through engagement-frustration. Reform would be suicidal for them. No dominant actor can pivot without self-sabotage. ALMA settles on empty territory.

02

EU-native compliance

GDPR, DSA, AI Act. Our architecture is designed from the start to comply with these frameworks, where our American competitors struggle to retrofit. Advantage growing over time.

03

Unique long-term data

We are the only ones who take users all the way to physical meeting and then follow the outcome. This creates a data asset impossible to reproduce by platforms that stop at the "match."

04

Defensible ethical brand

Our public positioning, our transparency commitments, our community under construction create a brand that no fast-follower can copy by pasting communication. It's a cultural asset.
