— The founder —

Hamza El Jawahiry. 22, and a conviction.

The story of an intuition that hasn't faded in three years, and that now takes shape in ALMA.

My name is Hamza El Jawahiry. I am 22 years old. I come from the generation sometimes called Z — the one that grew up with a smartphone in hand, that discovered love at the age of Instagram, and that learned to connect with others through screens before learning to do so face to face.

Here is why I am devoting the next years of my life to trying to repair something that was broken without anyone really noticing.

The trigger

About three years ago, I started noticing something around me that deeply troubled me. Male friends — brilliant, funny, sensitive people — who were gradually becoming bitter. Not at once, not violently. Slowly. Month after month, swipe after swipe, they accumulated a feeling of not existing. Of being worth only what their photo was worth. Some ended up developing a terrible resentment toward women, explaining to themselves that women only saw numbers or appearances in them.

And in parallel, female friends I love — intelligent, open, generous — who were transforming into something harder. Worn down by inappropriate messages, harassment, dates that ended badly. Eventually, some said they no longer trusted a man without proof.

The worst part of this story is that they didn't even know each other. But a technology had managed to make them resent each other in advance. They were the crossed victims of a system that was destroying them both, in different ways.

The understanding

I tried to understand how this was possible. I read Sherry Turkle, who documented decades ago how digital tools were changing the way we relate. I read Jonathan Haidt, who shows how social platforms and apps structurally undermine the psychological development of younger generations. I read Esther Perel, who reminds us that intimacy is a practice, not a transaction.

Then I discovered the numbers. 63% of men under 30 in the United States are now single. Compared to 34% of women in the same age range. This gender gap was 8 points in 2010 — it is 29 points today. On dating apps, the majority of women's messages go to a small minority of men. It's mathematical, it's documented, and it's a generational catastrophe.

Tinder generates billions of dollars in revenue per year. How? By keeping its users frustrated, single, trapped in the game.

If you find love, you leave, and Tinder loses a customer. So everything — algorithms, notifications, premium tiers, design — is optimized to keep you in permanent search. An industry that thrives on the suffering it manufactures.

The decision

At that moment, I understood two things. First: this problem will never be solved by the incumbents. Match Group cannot become ethical without killing its own business. Wall Street shareholders would not allow it. The system is locked from the inside.

Second, more personal: if I didn't do it, who would? I was 22, I lived the problem, I had the cold rage you need to carry this kind of fight over time. And I had that particular intuition of my generation that older people couldn't have.

So I made the decision. I paused everything else I was doing. I started building.

The conviction

I'm 22. I don't have twenty years of experience. I don't have another exit behind me. But I am exactly the age of my target, I live the problem, and I commit to carrying this project as far as it takes for my generation to recover its capacity to love.

I know many will tell me it's naive. That the apps are unsalvageable. That no one will pay for a meeting when Tinder is free. That my generation doesn't want long-term. That you don't change a fifty-billion-dollar industry with a good idea.

I'd rather be naive and try than cynical and look away.

My personal mission

ALMA is not just a startup. For me, it is the embodiment of a broader conviction: technology can serve human connection instead of exploiting it. But that requires designing against the grain of the attention economy.

My ten-year goal is clear: that ALMA be recognized as the actor that enabled an entire generation to recover its capacity to love lastingly. That other companies follow our example in other markets where extractive models have done damage. And personally, that one day we can measure the number of couples and families that exist thanks to what we will have built.

I know it will be hard. I know I'll make mistakes. I know I'll have doubts. I know I'll be attacked, criticized, sometimes discouraged. But I also know why I'm doing it.

Long-term love has become revolutionary in the age of the disposable. ALMA is the app that owns that choice.

— Hamza El Jawahiry
